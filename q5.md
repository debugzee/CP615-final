## Assumptions used

Because some cardinalities and keys are not fully explicit in the EER diagram, the following reasonable assumptions were used:

- `Artist.Name` is the primary key of `Artist`
- `ArtObject.objectID` is the primary key of `ArtObject`
- `Event.Name` is the primary key of `Event`
- `Owner.Name` is the primary key of `Owner`
- `Period` is a weak entity identified by its owning `Temporary` art object together with `StartDate`
- `Creates` is mapped as **1:N** from `Artist` to `ArtObject`
- `Shown at` is mapped as **M:N** between `ArtObject` and `Event`
- `Borrowed from` is mapped as **1:N** from `Owner` to `Period`

---

# 2. Mapping of each relationship to the relational model

## A. `Creates` between `Artist` and `Art Object`

### Choice made

Mapped by placing a foreign key in `ART_OBJECT`.

### Relational mapping

- `ARTIST(Name PK, Epoch, Country)`
- `ART_OBJECT(objectID PK, Title, Year, Description, artistName FK -> ARTIST.Name)`

### Justification

This relationship is best interpreted as **one artist creates many art objects**, while each art object is created by one artist. Since the relationship has no attributes of its own, the standard mapping is to place the primary key of `ARTIST` as a foreign key in `ART_OBJECT`.

---

## B. `Shown at` between `Art Object` and `Event`

### Choice made

Mapped as a separate relationship table.

### Relational mapping

- `EVENT(Name PK, Date, Venue)`
- `SHOWN_AT(objectID FK -> ART_OBJECT.objectID, eventName FK -> EVENT.Name, PK(objectID, eventName))`

### Justification

This relationship is naturally **many-to-many**:

- one art object may be shown at many events
- one event may include many art objects

For an M:N relationship, the correct relational mapping is a separate associative relation containing the primary keys of both participating entities.

---

## C. `until` between `Temporary` and `Period`

### Choice made

Mapped by making `Period` a weak relation whose key includes the key of `Temporary`.

### Relational mapping

- `TEMPORARY(objectID PK/FK -> ART_OBJECT.objectID, Insurance, Reason)`
- `PERIOD(temporaryObjectID FK -> TEMPORARY.objectID, StartDate, EndDate, ownerName FK -> OWNER.Name, PK(temporaryObjectID, StartDate))`

### Justification

`Period` is shown as a **weak entity** and `until` is its **identifying relationship** with `Temporary`. Therefore, the primary key of the owner entity `Temporary` must appear in `PERIOD`, and it must also be part of the primary key of `PERIOD`.

`StartDate` is used as the discriminator/partial key to uniquely distinguish periods for the same temporary object.

---

## D. `Borrowed from` between `Period` and `Owner`

### Choice made

Mapped by placing a foreign key to `OWNER` in `PERIOD`.

### Relational mapping

- `OWNER(Name PK, Address)`
- `PERIOD(..., ownerName FK -> OWNER.Name, ...)`

### Justification

This relationship is most reasonably interpreted as **one owner can be associated with many borrowing periods**, but each borrowing period refers to one owner. Since the relationship has no attributes, the simplest mapping is to place the owner's key in `PERIOD`.

---

# 3. Mapping of specialization/generalization structures

Although these are not ordinary named relationships, they must also be mapped into the relational model.

## A. `Event` -> `Exhibition`, `Auction` (overlapping)

### Relational mapping

- `EVENT(Name PK, Date, Venue)`
- `EXHIBITION(Name PK/FK -> EVENT.Name, Theme, Curator)`
- `AUCTION(Name PK/FK -> EVENT.Name, EstimatedValue, SalePrice)`

### Justification

A separate relation is created for the supertype and for each subtype. This works well for **overlapping specialization**, because the same event may appear in both `EXHIBITION` and `AUCTION`.

---

## B. `Art Object` -> `Temporary`, `Permanent` (disjoint, total)

### Relational mapping

- `ART_OBJECT(objectID PK, ...)`
- `TEMPORARY(objectID PK/FK -> ART_OBJECT.objectID, Insurance, Reason)`
- `PERMANENT(objectID PK/FK -> ART_OBJECT.objectID, Cost, DateAcquired)`

### Justification

Using one relation for the supertype and one for each subtype preserves the subtype-specific attributes clearly. Disjointness and total participation are semantic constraints that are usually enforced through assertions, checks, or application logic.

---

## C. `Art Object` -> `Painting`, `Sculpture` (disjoint)

### Relational mapping

- `PAINTING(objectID PK/FK -> ART_OBJECT.objectID, Style, PaintType)`
- `SCULPTURE(objectID PK/FK -> ART_OBJECT.objectID, Weight, Material)`

### Justification

The same subtype mapping method is used here. Each subtype relation reuses the parent key as both primary key and foreign key.

---

## D. `Sculpture` -> `Statue`

### Relational mapping

- `STATUE(objectID PK/FK -> SCULPTURE.objectID, Name, DateBorn, DateDied)`

### Justification

`Statue` is a subtype of `Sculpture`, so the subtype table uses the key of `SCULPTURE` as both its primary key and foreign key.

---

# 4. Mapping of multivalued and composite attributes

## A. `Artist.Styles` (multivalued)

### Relational mapping

- `ARTIST_STYLE(artistName FK -> ARTIST.Name, Style, PK(artistName, Style))`

### Justification

A multivalued attribute must be mapped to a separate relation containing the key of the owning entity and the multivalued attribute.

---

## B. `Exhibition.Sponsors` (multivalued)

### Relational mapping

- `EXHIBITION_SPONSOR(eventName FK -> EXHIBITION.Name, Sponsor, PK(eventName, Sponsor))`

### Justification

This is also a multivalued attribute, so it is mapped to its own relation.

---

## C. `Statue.Life time` (composite)

### Relational mapping

- stored as:
  - `DateBorn`
  - `DateDied`

### Justification

Composite attributes are decomposed into their simple components in the relational model.

---

# 6. Justification of uniqueness and nullability choices

## Foreign keys that are **Both** unique and not null

These are the foreign keys used in subtype tables:

- `EXHIBITION.Name`
- `AUCTION.Name`
- `TEMPORARY.objectID`
- `PERMANENT.objectID`
- `PAINTING.objectID`
- `SCULPTURE.objectID`
- `STATUE.objectID`

### Why?

Because in each subtype relation, the foreign key to the parent is also the primary key of the subtype relation. A primary key must be:

- unique
- not null

So these foreign keys must satisfy both conditions.

---

## Foreign keys that are **Not null**

These include:

- `ART_OBJECT.artistName`
- `ARTIST_STYLE.artistName`
- `SHOWN_AT.objectID`
- `SHOWN_AT.eventName`
- `EXHIBITION_SPONSOR.eventName`
- `PERIOD.temporaryObjectID`
- `PERIOD.ownerName`

### Why?

They must not be null because either:

- they are part of a primary key, or
- the participation/ownership meaning of the EER indicates that the related entity must exist

They are not unique because the relationship on that side is one-to-many or many-to-many.

---

## Foreign keys that are **Neither**

Under the assumptions used here, none of the foreign keys are classified as "neither."

If a stricter interpretation is used where only explicitly shown total participation implies `NOT NULL`, then `ART_OBJECT.artistName` and `PERIOD.ownerName` could be argued to be nullable. In that alternative interpretation, those two foreign keys would become **Neither**.

---

# 7. Final relational schema

```text
ARTIST(
    Name PK,
    Epoch,
    Country
)

ARTIST_STYLE(
    artistName PK/FK -> ARTIST.Name,
    Style PK
)

ART_OBJECT(
    objectID PK,
    Title,
    Year,
    Description,
    artistName FK -> ARTIST.Name
)

EVENT(
    Name PK,
    Date,
    Venue
)

SHOWN_AT(
    objectID PK/FK -> ART_OBJECT.objectID,
    eventName PK/FK -> EVENT.Name
)

EXHIBITION(
    Name PK/FK -> EVENT.Name,
    Theme,
    Curator
)

EXHIBITION_SPONSOR(
    eventName PK/FK -> EXHIBITION.Name,
    Sponsor PK
)

AUCTION(
    Name PK/FK -> EVENT.Name,
    EstimatedValue,
    SalePrice
)

TEMPORARY(
    objectID PK/FK -> ART_OBJECT.objectID,
    Insurance,
    Reason
)

PERMANENT(
    objectID PK/FK -> ART_OBJECT.objectID,
    Cost,
    DateAcquired
)

PAINTING(
    objectID PK/FK -> ART_OBJECT.objectID,
    Style,
    PaintType
)

SCULPTURE(
    objectID PK/FK -> ART_OBJECT.objectID,
    Weight,
    Material
)

STATUE(
    objectID PK/FK -> SCULPTURE.objectID,
    Name,
    DateBorn,
    DateDied
)

OWNER(
    Name PK,
    Address
)

PERIOD(
    temporaryObjectID PK/FK -> TEMPORARY.objectID,
    StartDate PK,
    EndDate,
    ownerName FK -> OWNER.Name
)
```
